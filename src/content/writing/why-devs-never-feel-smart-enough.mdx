I. The Hook
~150 words
Open with the Sunday evening scene — specific, physical, recognisable. Not "developers often feel overwhelmed by the pace of change" but the actual moment: the Mastodon scroll, three new things you haven't heard of, the Slack message with "have you seen this?", the quiet arithmetic of things you don't know yet adding up faster than the things you're learning.
Don't name the feeling yet. Let the reader confirm they've been there first.
Then land one sentence that reframes it before the reader has time to assume this is another productivity post: the problem isn't the pace of change. It's the measuring stick.

II. The Imposter Tax — Four Ways It Shows Up
~300 words
Name and describe the four anxiety sources from the live post, but reframe each one as a symptom of the fictional-target problem rather than as standalone fears. Each one should feel like evidence accumulating toward the thesis, not a listicle.
The Perpetual Horizon — the feeling that mastering one area just reveals five more you haven't touched. Every new spec, every new framework, every "you should look at X" from a colleague. The horizon doesn't get closer because it isn't a horizon — it's a moving target set by the feed, not by the job.
The Codebase Blind Spot — the fear of being dropped into a system you didn't write. Worth treating honestly here: this one is real and won't be fixed by mindset. But the anxiety it creates (I should already know this) is distinct from the actual task (I need to learn this now). The fictional target says you should have pre-learned every system. The real job says you learn the one in front of you.
The Stack Overflow Reliance — the self-doubt about reaching for external help, documentation, or references. This is worth a careful sentence or two: looking things up isn't a failure of memory, it's the correct behaviour. The anxiety comes from treating recall as the metric. Nobody reasonable does.
The Public Exposure — the fear of visible correction. A PR comment, a team meeting, a reply from someone with more followers. This is the most social of the four and the most disproportionate in how much cognitive load it takes up relative to how often it actually happens.
Don't resolve any of these yet. End the section with a single observation: all four of them share the same structure — a gap between what you know and what you imagine you should know. The gap is real. The "should" is invented.

III. The Fictional Target
~300 words
This is the core section — the argument that makes the post worth writing.
The feeling of "not keeping up" is based on a target that doesn't exist. There is no version of you that is current on everything — not because you're falling short of a real standard, but because the surface area of frontend development is now genuinely too large for any one person to cover.
Make this concrete. Don't just assert it. Name the disciplines: CSS, JavaScript, tooling, accessibility, performance, security, AI integration, browser APIs, framework ecosystems — each of these is a full-time specialism. The developer who was "full stack" in 2012 was covering a substantially smaller stack than the developer trying to be "full stack" today. The job title stayed the same. The scope didn't.
The discomfort isn't a signal that you're behind. It's a signal that you're measuring yourself against a 2012-sized obligation using 2026's surface area. The measuring stick is wrong.
This connects to but is distinct from the Imposter Syndrome post — reference it briefly, one sentence. That post is about doubting whether you belong. This one is about realising the map you've been navigating by was drawn wrong to begin with. You can resolve imposter syndrome and still feel this anxiety, because they have different roots.

IV. Why the Feed Makes It Worse
~250 words
The mechanics of how social feeds systematically distort the picture — and why the distortion feels like reality.
The recency bias problem. What appears in your feed is what was published recently and shared widely — which skews heavily toward new things. Stable, working, well-understood technology is invisible on social media because nobody posts about the thing that's been quietly fine for three years. The feed gives you a systematically distorted picture of what the industry is actually using day to day. Most production codebases are not running the thing that's trending on Bluesky this week.
Announcement vs adoption. There's a significant lag between when something is announced and when it's genuinely worth learning in depth. The feed collapses that distance and makes everything feel equally urgent. It isn't. Be honest about your own experience here — a specific feature you followed on announcement and only actually learned when a real project required it. The GSAP → scroll animations post is a usable example if it fits.
The invisibility of expertise. The people who are genuinely expert in something are usually building with it, not posting about it. The feed over-represents the newly-curious and under-represents the deeply-knowledgeable. This creates a false impression of how much everyone else knows and how quickly they came to know it.

V. Selective Ignorance as a Professional Skill
~300 words
The reframe. Not keeping up with everything isn't a failure mode — it's a strategy. The distinction is whether it's deliberate or accidental.
Knowing what you're ignoring and why. There's a difference between "I haven't looked at that yet" (passive, guilt-adjacent) and "I've decided that's not relevant to my work right now" (active, professional judgment). Making that second move explicitly — even just in your own head — changes the relationship from guilt to agency. You're not behind. You made a call.
Trusting your work to surface what you need. Some of the best learning happens when a real problem pulls you toward a solution, rather than when you push yourself toward knowledge in the abstract. The imposter syndrome post gestures at this — the Notion draft pile, the posts you decided not to write. The same principle applies to learning: not everything in the feed needs to become a project. Real work is a better filter than a reading list.
Following people rather than topics. Topic-based follows optimise for volume. Following specific people whose judgment you trust optimises for signal. A smaller, more considered feed of people who think carefully about things you care about is more useful than a comprehensive one.
The garden as a filter. If something is worth writing about, it's worth learning. If it's not worth writing about — not yet, not for this audience, not for your current work — maybe it doesn't need to be learned right now. The Page Turners series already does this implicitly: the act of deciding what goes in the roundup is itself a filtering practice. Name that.

VI. The DevRel Wrinkle
~150 words
The section that makes this post specifically honest for your positioning. Developer advocates are professionally expected to know what's new — it's arguably in the job description. So how do you square "selective ignorance as a skill" with a role that rewards staying current?
The answer is that DevRel requires curation, not comprehensiveness. The developer advocate who knows five things deeply and can explain them clearly to a room is more valuable than the one who has skimmed fifty things and can name-drop all of them. The output of the work — talks, posts, demos, conversations — demands depth. The feed still isn't the job. The audience is.
This is also worth being honest about: the anxiety of "not keeping up" doesn't go away in DevRel. It might be louder. But the answer is the same: the fictional target is still fictional, even when it feels more professionally loaded.

VII. What "Enough" Actually Looks Like
~200 words
Practical and concrete — but not a prescriptive system. Those never fit everyone. Instead, a small set of questions worth sitting with:

Do I know the things my current work actually requires of me?
Am I aware of the direction the platform is heading, even if I'm not current on every detail?
When something genuinely new comes across my path, do I have a way to decide whether to go deeper or park it?
Can I have a conversation about the state of the frontend ecosystem without feeling like a fraud?

If the answer to most of those is yes, you're keeping up enough. The rest is noise the feed has dressed as signal.
End with a specific, personal note — not advice, but your own current version of "enough." What are you deliberately not following right now? What have you parked? That specificity is what stops this from reading like a Medium productivity post. The personal detail is the whole thing.

VIII. Close
~100 words
Resist the tidy resolution. This isn't a solved problem — it's an ongoing negotiation. The feed will keep moving. New frameworks will keep appearing. Someone will always be posting about something you haven't heard of yet.
The goal isn't to win the keeping-up game. It's to stop playing it on the feed's terms.
